    GPU Rack

    Think of this like a computer case when building a PC. You need a special rack to hold 6 or more GPU's above the rest of the components.

    Motherboard

    You want a specialized mining motherboard with multiple PCIe slots. Any motherboard with extra PCIe slots should work, but mining specific motherboards are built for this purpose and may have special features that make a miners life easier.

    Power Supply

    We need a beefy power supply that can handle the load, and also has enough VGA slots for the number of cards you plan to install. 2 power supplies can be chainlinked together, but it's much cleaner to have 1 stronger power supply. You never want to exceed 80-85% of your power supplies maximum capacity. Example: 1000 watt power supply, try to stay under 850 watts total power draw.

    Processor

    Depending on the motherboard you choose, you can get an Intel Celeron or i3, or an AMD Ryzen 3000 series. The GPUs are going to be doing the heavy lifting, no need for a high end CPU. If you plan on doing more than 6 GPU, then we recommend going above a celeron (i3 or i5) for system stability and responsiveness, as the Celeron only has 2 cores and much less cache. Any modern Ryzen series CPU will have more than enough cores, they typically have 4+. With 6 or less GPUs, a Celeron will be suffice. Make sure the motherboard and processor are compatible. The motherboard description should tell you which generations and type of CPU are compatible.

    RAM

    8 GB of Memory can handle everything we need. You might find other documentation online that says you can get by with 4 GB. Maybe in the past 4 GB worked, but algorithms and software have since evolved and some coins are more resource intensive. The price difference is negligible and you get better system responsiveness and stability. We recommend going for 8 GB if you are building a 4-8 card rig, and possibly 16 GB if you are going higher than 8 cards. Make sure the memory is compatible with your motherboard and processor.

    SSD

    SSD or Solid State Hard Drives prices have come down so much, that there is no reason to get a regular hard drive. The system responsiveness and load time compared to traditional hard drives are in a different league. You may find documentation online that says you can get away with a 60 GB drive, but we recommend atleast 240-500 GB. With prices as low as they are there is no need to skimp on this and regret it later. You will need space reserved for virtual memory to match the amount of GPU memory you have. Also, you want room to install all your different mining software when you want to mine different coins. If you are using Windows, it will bog down or crash when it is running low on space.

    Risers

    This is what the GPU is directly hooked up to. They are relatively cheap, so we suggest always buying more than you need and keep a small inventory in case you need to switch some out if they go bad. These need to be powered from the power supply. It can be powered 3 different ways: Sata, Molex and VGA cable. You will find a lot of documentation online that strongly advise against SATA and Molex. There are reports of short circuit, fires, and melting wires. The general consensus is to hook up a VGA cable directly to provide power. However, from our personal experience, we have used SATA and have been mining for over 2 years without any issues. We only power a maximum of 2 Risers per Sata cable. Do your own research and proceed at your own risk.

    Power Button

    When you build a PC in a case, there is a power button on the case that you wire to the motherboard. This is basically the same thing. You could technically grab a screw driver and put it between the positive and negative power terminals and get the system started. The button only costs a couple of bucks, and some mining specific motherboards or racks come with one.

    Graphics Cards (GPUs)

    In our experience mining for over 2 years, our rigs with 6 cards and below were the most stable and problem free. We also recommend getting the exact same cards, it's just easier to manage, undervolt, overclock and overall setup. We also think the end products looks that much cleaner.
